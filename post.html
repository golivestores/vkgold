The Last Regong Masters: Guardians of a Dying Art

12 April 2026 8 min read VKGold Atelier

Tucked into a narrow valley where the Longwu River carves through the eastern Tibetan Plateau, the town of Regong sits at 2,500 metres above sea level in Qinghai province. Known in Tibetan as Rebgong, meaning "golden cradle," this remote settlement of roughly 30,000 people has produced some of the most extraordinary sacred art in human history. For over seven centuries, Regong has been the epicentre of Thangka painting, a tradition so culturally significant that UNESCO inscribed "Regong Arts" on its Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in 2009.

The recognition was well-earned but bittersweet. At the time of inscription, fewer than fifty master-level Thangka painters remained active in the valley. Today, that number has dwindled further. The younger generation, drawn to the cities by economic opportunity and digital connectivity, increasingly views the decade-long apprenticeship required to master this art as an impractical relic. What UNESCO sought to protect continues to slip quietly away, one retiring master at a time.

The Lineage of Quzhi and Zhaxijiancuo

Among the remaining masters, two names command particular reverence: Quzhi (曲智) and Zhaxijiancuo (扎西尖措). Both trace their artistic lineage directly to the Menlha school, the oldest continuous tradition of Thangka painting in Regong, established in the fourteenth century. Quzhi, now in his late sixties, began his training at age nine under his uncle, himself a master of the previous generation. He recalls grinding mineral pigments by hand before dawn, a task he performed daily for three years before he was permitted to touch a brush to canvas. Zhaxijiancuo, a generation younger at fifty-two, trained under Quzhi for twelve years and now leads the last formal atelier in the valley where apprentices still learn the traditional way.

The process of creating a single Thangka is painstaking beyond what most modern viewers can comprehend. A large work measuring one metre by 1.5 metres takes between six months and two years to complete. The canvas, made from hand-woven cotton, is first stretched and treated with a mixture of yak-skin glue and chalk. The master then sketches the composition using charcoal made from willow branches, following iconographic proportions codified in Buddhist texts that have remained unchanged for centuries. Every measurement -- the distance between a deity's eyes, the angle of a hand mudra, the ratio of a lotus petal to the full mandala -- must be mathematically precise. An error of even two millimetres is considered spiritually unacceptable.

"We do not paint for galleries or collectors. Each stroke is a prayer, each colour an offering. When I open the eyes of the Buddha, I am not finishing a painting -- I am inviting a consciousness to dwell within it." -- Master Quzhi, recorded during a 2024 interview in Regong

Pigments, Gold, and the Eye-Opening Ceremony

The pigments used in Regong Thangkas are derived entirely from minerals and organic materials sourced from the surrounding plateau. Azurite provides the iconic deep blue of Amitabha's robe. Malachite yields the green of Tara's skin. Cinnabar gives the red of sacred flames. These minerals are ground by hand with mortar and pestle, mixed with hide glue, and layered in thin washes -- sometimes thirty or more layers for a single colour field. The gold work, perhaps the most distinctive feature of Regong Thangkas, uses 24-karat gold leaf hammered to a thickness of 0.1 microns, applied with a brush made from the ear hair of a Himalayan yak calf. The gold lines that trace halos, flames, and textile patterns are drawn freehand, requiring a steady hand and complete breath control.

The final act of creation is the "eye-opening ceremony" (开眼), a ritual that transforms the Thangka from a decorative object into a sacred presence. Performed by the master painter in conjunction with a lama, the ceremony involves inscribing the pupils of the central deity at an astrologically auspicious moment. Until this moment, the painting is considered incomplete, a body without a soul. After the eyes are opened, the Thangka is consecrated with mantras written in gold on its reverse side and sealed with the lama's blessing. It is this ceremony that distinguishes a Regong Thangka from a decorative reproduction -- and it is this ceremony that cannot be replicated by a machine or an untrained hand.

VKGold's partnership with the Regong ateliers represents a deliberate effort to create a sustainable bridge between these masters and the collectors of Southeast Asia. By commissioning works directly from Quzhi, Zhaxijiancuo, and their senior apprentices, VKGold ensures that fair compensation reaches the artists who spend months or years on a single piece. Each Thangka shipped to Singapore arrives with full documentation of its provenance, the master's seal, a certificate of the eye-opening ceremony, and a detailed account of the pigments and gold used. It is not merely a purchase; it is participation in the preservation of a living tradition. As Master Zhaxijiancuo has said, "A Thangka that finds a devoted home is a Thangka that keeps our art alive."
